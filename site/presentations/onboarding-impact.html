Verdict: the flow got better for engaged users — but the headline rate can't see it.

These two numbers measure different denominators. Heap counts people who actually start the flow (3 in 4 finish — a healthy funnel). f_school_facts counts every free signup, most of whom are low‑intent and never begin. Progressive disclosure can lift the experience for people who engage while barely moving a blended rate dominated by no‑intent accounts. Both are true; report them side by side and lead with the conditional funnel when judging the design.

Verdict: a real lift, but don't hand it all to personalization.

Conversion rose from ~3.9% to ~4.7% (+22% relative) — the strongest of the three signals. The honest caveat: the climb started in early January, weeks before the personalization step shipped on Feb 16, which points to seasonality (new‑year buyers) doing part of the work. The personalization step plausibly helped, but this dataset can't separate its effect from the seasonal tailwind. The last two weeks are faded because those cohorts haven't had a full 14 days to convert yet.

Hypothesis 3 · Product creation

From a five‑step builder to one screen

Product creation collapsed from a multi‑step modal into a single "Create your first product" screen inside the flow. The bet: fewer steps → more people create a product during onboarding (and, downstream, convert).

Verdict: a modest, robust win — and a clean read.

In‑session product creation rose from 7.0% to 7.7% (+10% relative), and the result holds whether we define "in the flow" as 30 minutes, 1 hour, or a full day. Note too that almost all product creation that happens at all happens in that first session — which is exactly the behavior the one‑step change was meant to capture. We deliberately switched off the original "created within 14 days" metric here: it counts activation anytime in trial and would credit the redesign for things it didn't touch.

Data integrity

The March spike was fraud — here's how we know

For three weeks (Mar 8–22), completion and in‑session product creation roughly tripled, then snapped back to baseline. Taken at face value it looks like a smashing success. It isn't — it was a fraud spike, not real creators, and the cohort‑quality numbers fit that exactly.

Why a temporary spike ≠ a design win

A genuine flow improvement produces a sustained step‑change. This tripled for exactly three weeks and reverted. Fraudulent accounts fake their way through onboarding — even faking conversions and enrolling fake students — so they inflate completion, creation, and even conversion without representing real creators. Both the surge weeks and the secondary Apr 12 volume spike are excluded from every headline number on this page.

What this can & can't tell us

Directional, not yet causal

The signals point the right way — but observational cohort data over a staggered rollout can't fully separate the design from everything else moving at the same time.

  • 01Seasonality is unmodeled. New‑year and spring buying behavior shifted conversion independent of the redesign — visible in the January rise that predates personalization.
  • 02No holdout. Every signup saw the new flow once it shipped, so there's no concurrent control group to difference against.
  • 03Changes overlap. Mid‑ and end‑March shipped two weeks apart and collide with the fraud window, making the two March changes hard to isolate from each other.
  • 04Completion is denominator‑sensitive. The population rate and the Heap conditional rate tell different stories; neither alone is "the" completion number.
Where this points

What it means for the onboarding strategy

The data doesn't just grade three hypotheses — it tells us what onboarding should optimize for next. Three bets, ordered by how much the evidence backs them.

01
Reframe the goal

Optimize for activation among intenders — not blended completion

Make the lead metric "created a product in‑session AND converted within 14 days," measured on signups who signal intent (via the new step‑1 cards) — not a population completion rate swamped by people who never start. Route low‑intent signups to a lighter path so the guided flow isn't tuned for users who'll never build.

Why: population completion is flat (~15%) while in‑flow completion is ~75% — the blended number literally can't see the design working.
02
Proven lever — press it

Make in‑session product creation the spine of onboarding

Pull one‑step creation earlier and harder: keep "Generate with AI" as the default, pre‑fill product type and templates from the step‑1 intent answer, and strip the remaining fields. This is the behavior most tied to conversion, so lifting it lifts trials with it.

Why: H3 is the cleanest, most robust win (+10%, stable from 30 min to 24 hr), and nearly all product creation that happens at all already happens in that first session.
03
Promising — prove it first

Isolate personalization before scaling it

Don't pour resources into more branding moments yet. Run a holdout to get a causal read; if it confirms, extend the name / domain / brand thread through onboarding and into the day‑0 home. If it doesn't, redirect that effort to Bet 02.

Why: conversion rose +22%, but the climb started in January — before personalization shipped — so seasonality is entangled with the effect.
Now

Unblock the truth

  • Launch a randomized holdout (≈10% kept on legacy) — the one move that makes every claim causal.
  • Stand up a warehouse‑native activation funnel, with fraud filtering on by default.
  • Redefine the north‑star: in‑session creation + 14‑day conversion among intenders.
Next

Press the proven lever

  • Intent‑based product templates, pre‑filled from step 1.
  • Bring the creation prompt earlier in the flow.
  • Trim the remaining fields on the create screen.
Later

Scale what's confirmed

  • If the holdout confirms it, extend personalization across onboarding + the day‑0 home.
  • Build a low‑intent "fast path" so the guided flow stays focused on builders.
★
If you do one thing: ship the randomized holdout. It's cheap, it removes the seasonality and no‑control confounds that cap every finding here, and it turns this retrospective into a measurement system that grades the next change in weeks — not months.
